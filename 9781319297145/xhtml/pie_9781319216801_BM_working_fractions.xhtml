Working with Fractions: A Review

Fractions are commonly used in working genetics problems because we are often concerned with probabilities and with the proportions of progeny resulting from genetic crosses. At some point in the past, you undoubtedly learned how to manipulate fractions, but perhaps that was a long time ago. The following brief review is designed to help you recall some basic operations with fractions.

Characteristics of Fractions

What is a fraction? You probably remember that a fraction is used to represent a proportion, or part of a whole. It is a number presented in the form ab, sometimes written as ab. The top part of the fraction (the part above the line, a in this case) is called the numerator. The bottom part of the fraction (the part below the line, b in this case) is called the denominator.

We can think of the fraction ab as a out of b, as in 3 out of 4(34) of the progeny of a cross have yellow seeds. Another way to think of the fraction ab is that it means a divided by b. For example, 34=3÷4=0.75. We can always convert a fraction into a decimal number by simply dividing the numerator by the denominator.

In simple fractions, which are the kind most often used in genetics problems, a and b are integers—positive or negative whole numbers. A simple fraction might be 34 or 7125, but not 0.71.5. Also, remember that the denominator of a fraction cannot be zero because the fraction ab means that a is divided by b, and a number cannot be divided by zero. You will probably also remember that any number divided by itself is 1, so any fraction with the same number as the numerator and the denominator is equal to 1:

22=1.077=1.01616=1.0

The size of a fraction depends on both the numerator and the denominator. If the numerators of two fractions are the same but the denominators differ, the fraction with the larger denominator is smaller. Thus, 38 (=0.375) is smaller than 34 (=0.750). The opposite is true of the numerator. If the denominators of two fractions are the same but the numerators differ, the fraction with the larger numerator is larger. For example, 1416 (=0.875) is larger than 316 (=0.188).

Equivalent Fractions

Now that we’ve discussed some of the features of fractions, let’s review equivalent fractions. The same number can be represented by many different fractions. For example, the fractions 12, 24, and 48 all equal 0.5. These fractions, which have different numbers in the numerator and the denominator but are numerically equivalent, are called equivalent fractions. The proportions represented by equivalent fractions are the same:

The first fraction is 1 over 2 equals 0 point 5. Beside this, a rectangular block is shown divided into two equal parts; one part is shaded. The second fraction is 2 over 4 equals 0 point 5. Beside this, a rectangular block is shown divided into four equal parts; two parts are shaded. The third fraction is 4 over 8 equals 0 point 5. Beside this, a rectangular block is shown divided into eight equal parts; four parts are shaded. All the rectangular blocks are of same size.

We can produce equivalent fractions by multiplying both the numerator and the denominator by the same number, which is the same as multiplying by one:

12=1×22×2=24=0.5
24=2×24×2=48=0.5

In this way, we can create fractions that have the same denominator. This ability will be important when we need to add and subtract fractions (see Adding and Subtracting Fractions below). For example, suppose we want to convert 13 and 14 into equivalent fractions, both with the same denominator. We could multiply 13 by 44 and 14 by 33, so that both proportions now have 12 in the denominator:

13=1×43×4=412
14=1×34×3=312

The equivalent fraction 412 is equal in value to the original fraction (13) but has 12 in the denominator. Likewise, 312 is equal in value to the original fraction (14) but also has 12 in the denominator.

Simplifying Fractions

We often want to put fractions into their simplest equivalent forms. Simplifying fractions, for example, may help us to see that two fractions are equivalent.

To simplify a fraction, the numerator and denominator must first be factored. A factor is a number that the original number can be divided by evenly with no remainder. For example, 4 is a factor of 12 because 4 will divide into 12 exactly 3 times (124=3). However, 5 is not a factor of 12 because 5 cannot divide into 12 evenly (125=2.4). Thus, 4 can be factored as 4 = 2 × 2. Similarly, 8 can be factored as 8 = 2 × 2 × 2, and 15 can be factored as 15 = 3 × 5.

Fractions can be simplified when the numerator and denominator have a common factor:

416=4×14×4

Because 44=1, the 4s in both the numerator and denominator cancel out:

416=4×14×4=14

Thus, 416 can be simplified to 14.

Now that we’ve discussed some features of fractions, let’s review how we carry out simple mathematical operations with fractions, such as addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. These are the primary operations you will need to use as you work genetics problems.

Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Adding and subtracting fractions is easy, but first they must have the same denominator. To add two or more fractions with the same denominator, add the numerators and place the sum over the common denominator:

14+14=24
38+28+18=68

Similarly, to subtract two or more fractions with the same denominator, subtract the numerators and place the result over the common denominator:

68−18=581316−316−116=916

When the fractions you want to add or subtract don’t have the same denominator, then you must convert them into equivalent fractions that have the same denominator (see Equivalent Fractions above). For example, to add 12 and 13, convert both fractions into equivalent fractions with the common denominator 6:

12=1×32×3=36
13=1×23×2=26

Once the fractions have the same denominator, they can be added by summing the numerators and putting the result over the common denominator:

36+26=56

The same is true when subtracting fractions—they must have the same denominator. For example, to subtract 13 from 78, first convert the two fractions into equivalent fractions that have a common denominator, such as 24:

78=7×38×3=2124
13=1×83×8=824

Then subtract the numerators and put the result over the common denominator:

2124−824=1324

Multiplying and Dividing Fractions

Multiplying two or more fractions is simple. You multiply the numerators of the fractions and then multiply the denominators of the fractions:

12×34=1×32×4=38
34×34=3×34×4=916
12×34×34=1×3×32×4×4=932

Dividing fractions is a bit more complicated. Suppose we want to divide one fraction by another fraction, such as 14 divided by 34. First, take the reciprocal of the divisor (the second fraction, or 34). Taking the reciprocal means that you reverse the numbers in the numerator and the denominator. For example, the reciprocal of 34 is 43. Once you have the reciprocal, multiply the dividend (the first fraction, or 14) by the reciprocal of the divisor:

14×43=412

Another way to think about dividing fractions is to represent the original division (14 divided by 34) as a fraction itself, with 14 as the numerator and 34 as the denominator:

1434

To carry out the division, you multiply both the numerator and the denominator by the reciprocal of the denominator (43), which is the equivalent of multiplying by 1:

14×4334×43=4121212

The denominator now equals 1212, which is 1, so the equation simplifies to

4121212=4121=412

This is the same result we obtained when we multiplied the dividend by the reciprocal of the divisor.
