Chapter 14 Review

Chapter Summary

Section 14.1 Learning Objective: Be able to discuss how the concept of a gene changed as geneticists learned more about the molecular nature of genetic information.

  • A gene is often defined as a sequence of DNA nucleotides that is transcribed into a single RNA molecule, although the widespread occurrence and complexity of transcription makes this definition problematic.
  • Introns—noncoding sequences that interrupt the coding sequences (exons) of genes—are common in eukaryotic cells but rare in bacterial cells.

Section 14.2 Learning Objective: Be able to outline the different ways that mRNAs are modified in eukaryotic cells.

  • An mRNA molecule has three primary regions: a 5′ untranslated region, a protein-coding region, and a 3′ untranslated region.
  • Bacterial mRNA is translated immediately after transcription and undergoes little processing. The pre-mRNA of a eukaryotic protein-encoding gene is extensively processed: a modified guanine nucleotide and methyl groups, collectively termed the cap, are added to the 5′ end of pre-mRNA; the 3′ end is cleaved and a poly(A) tail is added; internal nucleotides are modified; and introns are removed.
  • Introns are removed from pre-mRNA within a structure called the spliceosome, which is composed of several small nuclear RNAs and proteins.
  • Some introns found in rRNA genes and mitochondrial genes are self-splicing.
  • Some pre-mRNAs undergo alternative processing, in which different combinations of exons are spliced together or different 3′ cleavage sites are used.
  • Messenger RNAs may be altered by the addition, deletion, or modification of nucleotides in the coding sequence, a process called RNA editing.

Section 14.3 Learning Objective: Be able to describe the structure and processing of tRNAs.

  • Transfer RNAs, which attach to amino acids, are short molecules that assume a common secondary structure and contain modified bases.
  • Transfer RNAs are processed by cleavage, trimming, and nucleotide addition and base modification.

Section 14.4 Learning Objective: Be able to compare the structures of prokaryotic and eukaryotic ribosomes and describe how rRNA is processed.

  • Ribosomes, the sites of protein synthesis, are composed of several ribosomal RNA molecules and numerous proteins.
  • Prokaryotic and eukaryotic ribosomes consist of small and large subunits but differ in the number of rRNA molecules and proteins.
  • Ribosomal RNA is extensively modified in both prokaryotes and eukaryotes.

Section 14.5 Learning Objective: Be able to describe the types of small RNAs and their functions.

  • Small interfering RNAs, microRNAs, Piwi-interacting RNAs, and CRISPR RNAs play important roles in gene silencing and in a number of other biological processes.

Section 14.6 Learning Objective: Be able to describe lncRNAs and their functions.

  • Long noncoding RNAs are RNA molecules that do not encode proteins. Evidence increasingly suggests that many of these molecules function in the control of gene expression.
  • Circular noncoding RNAs are common in many cells; some may serve as decoys for miRNAs.

Worked Problems

Problem 1

DNA from a eukaryotic gene was isolated, denatured, and hybridized to the mRNA transcribed from the gene; the hybridized structure was then observed with an electron microscope. The adjoining diagram shows the structure that was observed.

  1. How many introns and exons are there in this gene? Explain your answer.
  2. Identify and label the introns and exons in this hybridized structure.

Solution Strategy

What information is required in your answer to the problem?

  1. The number of introns and exons and how you arrived at your answer.
  2. The location of the introns and exons labeled on the figure.

What information is provided to solve the problem?

  • The DNA and mRNA are from a eukaryote.
  • The DNA was denatured and hybridized to the mRNA.
  • A diagram of the hybridized structure.

For help with this problem, review:

Introns in Section 14.1 and Figure 14.2.

Solution Steps

  1. Each of the loops represents a region in which sequences in the DNA do not have corresponding sequences in the RNA; these regions are introns. There are five loops in the hybridized structure so there must be five introns in the DNA and six exons.

    Recall: Introns are noncoding sequences found within eukaryotic genes.

  2. The drawing shows a wavy line with 3 loops above the line and 2 loops below it. The straight sections of the line between loops are labeled as exons and the loops extending outward from this straight line are labeled as introns.

    Hint: The number of introns will be one less than the number of exons.
